A5: Getting Meshy, and Lamps

Last updated: Tues, Nov 8, 2022

For this assignment, I practiced combining, checking and repairing meshes to
produce a single printable STL that passes checks for rapid prototyping.

I also ideated a lamp using digital fabrication techniques I've learned thus far.



---

I. Practicing with Meshes

When working with meshes, I kept the following in mind:

  • Polygon complexity — is there an appropriate amount of polygons in the mesh
    that keeps it lightweight yet detailed?
  • Intersections and holes — are the meshes closed with no naked edges?


I searched the web for existing STLs to play around with, and I thought it would be fun
to attempt to replicate the look of this character from a video game I had just started.



I found an STL of a human face as well as an STL of a replica of the character's mouthpiece.
I love how absolutely cursed these meshes look.




I did some initial checks with each mesh using the Rhino command MeshRepair.


Surprisingly, the mesh of the face was "a good mesh", but I anticipated the high
polygon complexity would make it a little difficult to work with so I repeatedly used
the command ReduceMesh to simplify the faces from 40,500 to 10,000.


I then checked the mesh of the mouthpiece which was so complex it made my computer slow.
MeshRepair indicated that the mesh was bad because it had many pairs of faces that intersect each other.


To fix this, I drastically reduced the mesh faces from 719,344 to 1,216.


With some additional scaling and stretching, I was able to piece these two good meshes together.
I also used the command PointsOn to select and move vertices to further connect the two meshes.
I then used the command MeshBooleanUnion to join the face and the mouthpiece into one closed mesh.




I exported the mesh as an STL file, then imported it into Cura to test its printing capabilities.
With supports, it's crying.
