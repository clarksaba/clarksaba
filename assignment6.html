A6: Casting and molding

Last updated: Tues, Nov 15, 2022

For this assignment, I created pawns using meta-molding techniques.

This was achieved by 3D printing a mold for an oomoo silicone mold, which
was then used to fill with hydrostone plaster to create identical pieces.

I plan to make a chess set for my final project where the pieces are created
using molding techniques, so I found this project particularly useful (still challenging).



---

I. Fabricating a 3D mold for an oomoo mold

I searched on Thingiverse and downloaded a pawn stl to use for this project.
My process for 3D modeling the mold can be seen below. I may have overcomplicated
the process because for some reason I struggled to figure out how to create keys with depth.

The main commands I used were:

  • MeshSplit to cut the pawn piece in half using a planar surface.
  • Split, BooleanUnion and BooleanDifference to subract and trim non-mesh
    surfaces and shape the mold box.



For my first failed print, I went with super quality and 0% infill (my mistake).
This is when I learned that Rhino doesn't fill in any non-mesh surfaces which
causes Cura to see those flat elevated surfaces as unprintable.



For my second print, I went with low quality and 20% infill to provide some
internal support for the elevated surface that the pawn would rest on.



While the low quality print may have created some rough surfaces for the mold,
I decided to move forward anyway due to time constraints.

When I repeat this process for my final project, I'll print in super quality.




---

II. Creating a silicone mold with oomoo

I mixed my oomoo silicone mixture and poured it into the printed molds.




After 6 hours, my silicone molds were done curing. I was surprised that the
rectangular keys I designed were a perfect fit since I saw many others were
using spheres for their keys. I secured the two molds together with rubber bands.





---

III. Casting duplicate pawn pieces with hydrostone

At this point, I had instantly learned my lesson: an air vent for the plaster
would've been extremely useful and might've helped create less of a mess.
Because my molds were really small too, I struggled to effectively pour the
mixture into the mold.

I continued to experiment with different hydrostone part mixtures to see the
differences it would create in the texture and quality of the casted pawn.

For the first mixture, I used nearly equal parts water and powder. This resulted
in a brittle material that almost caused the pawn to crack in half.



For the following mixtures, I gradually decreased the amount of water for a thicker
mixture. These took less time to cure and yielded more solid pawns than before.
The first few pawns with more water were brittle and tended to crack easily.



After these experiments, I will make the following adjustments for my final project:

  • Design air vents when creating the 3D printed mold.
  • Slice the 3D printed mold in super quality.
  • Use less water when casting with plaster.
