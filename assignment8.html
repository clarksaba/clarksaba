A8: Digitally fabricating a chess set

Last updated: Mon, Dec 5, 2022

For my final project, I used a combination of digital fabrication techniques to build a chess set including
the board and the pieces. The methods I used included:

  • Laser cutting, sanding and finishing
  • 3D modeling and mesh editing in Rhino
  • Slicing and 3D printing in Cura
  • Designing interference fits
This plan slightly deviates from my proposal in that I originally intended to use molding and casting techniques
to create all of the chess pieces. I moved away from this method because of how much time and material it would cost
to cast 64 pieces total. Oomoo and resin is quite expensive and I'm a poor college student.

My original plan also included subtractive manufacturing to construct the frame of the chess board, but I chose to
instead print the frame as one unit since I was downscaling the dimensions of the board anyway. Because I was creating
a mini-chess set, I added interference fits as one of my methods to cleanly press fit the pieces to the board.



---

I. Conceptualizing the full chess set in Rhino


To start, I modeled the exterior frame which would later be 3D printed. The purpose of 3D printing the frame
is to carry the wooden interior and provide a bevel accent that might not be easily achievable with laser cutting.

BooleanUnion helped me combine walls that were 3.5mm thick, and a base that was 1mm thick. To create the bevel
near the top, I BooleanDifference'd large cylinders to subtract edges from the walls.

The height of the frame was determined by the 1/8in laser cut wood panels I planned to insert inside.
A 17.5mm height provided room for a 1mm base, 5 layers of 3.2mm wood, and wiggle room for glue between layers.


The purpose of laser cutting wood for the interior and the top squares was to explore woodworking via digital fabrication.
Also, 3D printing panels takes a long time, so laser cutting wood was always the more time-efficient alternative.
I modeled how 5 layers of 1/8in wood look when stacked and glued together.

Because my chess frame design would be much smaller than a traditional chess board, I imagined so many moving
pieces on a small surface might be messy. Therefore, I designed the top squares with small holes (3mm diameter)
and modified chess piece meshes to include ~2.95mm pegs at their bases so that they can be press-fit together.




---

II. Laser cutting the wood interior

I acquired two large sheets of 1/8in wood in contrasting colors — birch and hardboard. I had originally hoped to
use maple and walnut, but I couldn't source the materials in time for this project deadline so I worked with what
I could immediately find at the art store.

I exported the outlines from Rhino to Illustrator and laid them out on an artboard.


When experimenting with laser cutter settings, I stuck with 100 power and 40 frequency.
Starting at around 20 speed, I gradually lowered it until the laser pierced the wood.

The birch wood required a speed of 10, and the hardboard wood required a speed of 5.


After the first successful laser cut, I repeated the print job on the leftover space to create the interior
wood panels and extra top squares if needed. In total, the top surface would only need 32 squares of each color.


I tightly glued the wood layers and squares together. I then sanded the squares to get rid of the burn marks from
the laser and create a smooth even surface. After a little cleaning, I finished the surface by rubbing mineral oil.




---

III. 3D printing the exterior frame

Before 3D printing the frame, I worked concurrently with the wood interior assembly to create rapid prototypes of
walls that allowed the interior to be smoothly inserted. I knew that I had to experiment with tolerances and slight
resizing to get the pieces to fit together– not too tight or too loose.

I created these rapid prototypes by vertically splitting the frame after resizing the walls.


After messing with tolerances and adjusting the size millimeter by millimeter, I got the measurements just right.
I also had to rescale my original model to fit within the boundaries of the Ender 3 Pro– nearly pushing its boundaries.


19 hours later, the print finished and satifyingly fit the wood interior.





---

IV. Modifying chess piece meshes, and testing interference fits

I sourced .stl meshes of chess pieces from Thingiverse and modified them to fit the dimensions of my particular board.

These modifications included scaling down the dimensions of the pieces and using ReduceMesh to simplify the polygons
from ~300,000 per piece to 3,000 polygons maximum so that the meshes would be easier to work with and output less lag.

Another modification was MeshBooleanUnion-ing ~2.95mm cylinder pegs at the base of each piece so that they could be
press fitted into the 3mm diameter holes that were laser cut across the square surfaces.


One major challenge I came across was figuring out what orientation to print each piece in.

Because I added a peg under each piece, printing the pieces upright would be unstable without proper support,
but the support often interfered with the peg itself and created inconsistent measurements for the pegs.
Printing pieces on the side had similar effects.

Printing pieces upside down with tree supports surprisingly yielded the most successful result with the
most consistently clean and precise pegs.


Bonus: a failed bulk print. I believe this was because some pieces like knights and bishops didn't automatically generate
proper supports. I proceeded by printing each piece individually and manually adding tree supports whenever needed.


It took a couple attempts and resizing to get the pegs properly press-fitted into the holes.
